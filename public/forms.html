Villanelle

19 lines · 2 rhymes · 2 refrains · 5 tercets + 1 quatrain

The villanelle is an obsession machine. Two whole lines — the refrains — return again and again, alternating as the final line of each tercet, until they collide as the closing couplet. Because only two rhyme sounds carry all nineteen lines, the form circles its subject relentlessly ("Do not go gentle into that good night" is the famous one).

Sestina

39 lines · no rhyme · 6 end-words · 6 sestets + 1 envoi

The sestina doesn't rhyme at all — instead it repeats the same six end-words, reordered stanza by stanza through a fixed spiral called retrogradatio cruciata: each new stanza takes the previous order and folds it outside-in (last, first, second-to-last, second…). After six stanzas every word has held every position; a three-line envoi then uses all six at once.

Sonnet (Shakespearean)

14 lines · 7 rhymes · iambic pentameter · 3 quatrains + couplet

Fourteen lines of iambic pentameter (ten syllables, da-DUM ×5). Three quatrains develop an idea in alternating rhyme; the final couplet lands the twist — the volta traditionally strikes at line 9 or in that last pair. Each rhyme sound is used exactly twice, so no single node is strained.

Ballad

quatrains · ABCB · alternating 8 / 6 syllables · any length

The ballad is poetry's storytelling engine — folk tales, murder ballads, sea songs. Quatrains alternate four-beat and three-beat lines (roughly 8 and 6 syllables), and only lines two and four need to rhyme, which keeps the narrative moving fast. A refrain stanza often returns like a chorus.

Limerick

5 lines · AABBA · anapestic bounce · disreputable

Five lines, two rhymes, and a punchline. Lines 1, 2, and 5 are long (three beats, 8–9 syllables) and rhyme together; lines 3 and 4 are short (two beats, 5–6 syllables) and rhyme with each other. The engine is the anapest — da-da-DUM — which is why limericks gallop.

Triolet

8 lines · 2 rhymes · line 1 appears three times

A miniature music box. Eight lines, but only about five of them are yours to write: line 1 returns as lines 4 and 7, and line 2 returns as line 8. The art is choosing a first line whose meaning shifts each time it comes back around.

Haiku

3 lines · 5 / 7 / 5 syllables · no rhyme · one turn

No rhyme, no meter — just seventeen syllables and a cut. The classical haiku sets an image, extends it, then pivots (the kireji, or cutting word) to a second image that reframes the first. Concreteness beats abstraction; seasons beat sentiments.
